First Law

‣ The First Law of Thermodynamics establishes the fundamental relationship between energy changes within a system and energy transfers across its boundaries. It states that the change in internal energy (ΔU) of a system equals the net heat transfer (Q) into the system minus the net work done (W) by the system. This principle represents a precise mathematical formulation of the conservation of energy, asserting that energy can neither be created nor destroyed, only transformed from one form to another or transferred between systems.


Mathematical Formulation

\[ \Delta U = Q - W \]

The change in internal energy (ΔU) represents the difference between the system's final and initial energy states:

\[ \Delta U = U_{final} - U_{initial} \]

Heat transfer (Q) is calculated as the integral of infinitesimal heat transfers:

\[ Q = \int \delta Q \]

Work done (W) is determined by pressure-volume work:

\[ W = \int P \, dV \]

Sign Convention

The sign convention in thermodynamics establishes a consistent framework for tracking energy transfers between a system and its surroundings. This convention is crucial for properly applying the First Law of Thermodynamics (ΔU = Q - W) and ensures universal understanding among scientists and engineers.

Standard Thermodynamic Sign Convention:
Heat Transfer (Q):
  • +Q: Heat added to the system from surroundings (endothermic process)
  • -Q: Heat released by the system to surroundings (exothermic process)
  • Measured in Joules (J) or calories (cal)
Work (W):
  • +W: Work done by the system on surroundings (expansion)
  • -W: Work done on the system by surroundings (compression)
  • For PV work: W = ∫PextdV

Biological Applications

Metabolic Energy Input

In biological systems, the first law manifests through metabolic processes. When organisms consume food, the digestive system breaks down complex molecules, converting their chemical potential energy into usable internal energy. This energy conversion can be expressed as:

\[ \Delta U = Q_{metabolism} - W_{biological} \]

Here, \( Q_{metabolism} \) quantifies the energy liberated through catabolic reactions, while \( W_{biological} \) encompasses all forms of biological work including muscle contraction, nerve impulse propagation, and biosynthesis.

Physical Activity Output

During physical exertion, the body's energy follows thermodynamic principles. The internal energy stored in ATP molecules converts to mechanical energy for muscle contraction, with significant energy loss as heat. This heat must be dissipated efficiently to prevent overheating during activities.

\[ \Delta U = -Q_{heat} - W_{mechanical} \]

This illustrates an energy output process, where \( Q_{heat} \) represents thermoregulation via sweating, and \( W_{mechanical} \) quantifies the work done by the musculoskeletal system.
